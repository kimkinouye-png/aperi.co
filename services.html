Services in detail

Ways to work together.

Four engagements on a spectrum, from a two-week diagnostic to fractional leadership. Each one is structured the same way: the problem in your words, what I actually do, and the artifacts you keep.

01

Operational diagnostic

Find where the work breaks before you spend on a fix.

Problem — how the client says it
  • Designers spend too much time in meetings.
  • Pods are in constant swirl, no clear action plan.
  • Stakeholders do not have transparency.
  • Stakeholders complain about not having design or requirements on time.
  • Engineering complains that files are not handoff ready.
  • Leadership can’t see who is working on what.
  • Can the current team fulfill this roadmap?
  • Do we have enough designers to execute?
  • When is this design going to be done?
What I do
  • Observe how teams work and catalogue their tools and methods end to end.
  • Pull metrics, or identify proxies, to measure success.
  • Map where your team’s workflows break.
  • Create a numbers-driven action plan to solve problem areas.
  • Implement solutions.
  • Track success.
Artifacts
  • Mapping of the team’s workflows
  • Points of leakage where teams waste cycles
  • Prioritized recommendations and action plan
  • Measurement plan and template
02

Ways of working setup

Intake, rhythms, and tooling configuration.

Problem — how the client says it
  • We bought Asana (or Jira), but no one uses it the same way, or at all.
  • Requests come from everywhere: Slack, hallway, email. They all turn into design work.
  • I can’t tell what my team is actually working on, or whether it’s the right work.
  • Designers can’t say no, so they’re buried and there’s no process to point to.
  • We keep adding meetings but nothing feels more aligned.
  • There’s no shared definition of when work is ready to start, or when it’s done.
  • Reviews happen ad hoc and decisions don’t stick.
  • Onboarding a new hire takes forever because none of this is written down.
What I do
  • Design a single intake process with a definition-of-ready gate, so undefined work can’t enter the queue.
  • Build the system of record inside your existing tools, so leadership can see who is working on what in one view.
  • Set up the operating rhythms — prioritization, design reviews, documentation, reporting — on a cadence built around how your team already works.
  • Roll rhythms out incrementally, one team at a time, starting with the team most ready for structure.
  • Run the system myself in the first weeks to prove value, then hand ownership to the team.
  • Define the workstream taxonomy, a sizing and resourcing method, and milestones.
  • Configure the tooling and integrations (Asana, Jira, Slack, Confluence, Figma).
Artifacts
  • Intake process with a definition-of-ready gate
  • System of record configured in your tools, with leadership portfolio views
  • Rhythm map: cadence, objective, and output for each meeting and ritual
  • Workstream taxonomy plus a sizing and resourcing method
  • Documentation and reporting templates
  • A short guide so the system survives onboarding and handoff
03

AI transformation and adoption

An operating model for AI that actually gets used.

Problem — how the client says it
  • We feel like we’re behind on AI and everyone else has figured it out.
  • Leadership handed us a mandate to use AI to move faster, but no one defined what faster means or how we’d know it worked.
  • Our process hasn’t actually changed. We’ve added AI tools on top of the same old workflow.
  • AI usage is all over the map. Some people are deep in it, others have never opened a tool.
  • We’re buying tools, but adoption stalls after the demo.
  • People are nervous the change means their job, so they quietly opt out.
  • Legal, risk, and compliance keep blocking the tools people actually want.
  • Pilots happen, then quietly die when the person running them gets busy.
What I do
Transformation: reinventing the PDLC
  • Define what acceleration means for your org before anyone touches a tool, broken down across your product development lifecycle.
  • Diagnose real friction through workshops and surveys, then redesign the lifecycle phases where AI changes the work — not just speeds up the old version of it.
  • Build a portfolio of pilots: sure-thing wins paired with riskier bets, with baselines and KPIs so each one proves or disproves itself.
Adoption and change management
  • Attach the AI mandate to pain people already feel, so the change is something they want rather than something done to them.
  • Drive adoption one incentive at a time, mapping why each person and their manager would trade hours for the new way of working.
  • Handle the change management: communication, addressing the job-security fear directly, and bringing managers in early so the shift sticks.
  • Navigate legal, risk, and compliance to get tools cleared for real use, through to general counsel sign-off.
Artifacts
  • A definition of what AI is accelerating, mapped to your redesigned lifecycle phases
  • Friction diagnostic from workshops and surveys
  • Pilot portfolio with prioritization (effort vs. return) and per-pilot measurement plans
  • Adoption and incentive map by stakeholder
  • Change management plan: comms, manager enablement, escalation path
  • Governance and legal-clearance path documented for repeat use
Proof
~80% Time saved across pilots in 6 months.
1st AI tool cleared through full legal, risk, and compliance review to general counsel sign-off.
04

Fractional / interim ops leadership

Run your ops function while you scale or decide whether to hire.

Problem — how the client says it
  • We need senior ops leadership now, but we’re not ready to commit to a full-time hire.
  • We’re scaling fast and the operational cracks are starting to show, but there’s no one whose job this is.
  • Our last ops leader left and work is falling through the gaps until we backfill.
  • We don’t actually know what to hire for yet. We need someone to define the role before we fill it.
  • Leadership is spending time on operational firefighting instead of strategy.
  • We have the budget for a fraction of a senior person, not a whole one.
What I do
  • Step in and run your ops function directly: planning, rhythms, resource allocation, and the day-to-day operating layer.
  • Build the operating system while I’m running it, so you’re not just renting coverage — you’re left with infrastructure that lasts.
  • Define what the permanent role should actually be, so when you hire, you hire against a real spec instead of a guess.
  • Absorb the operational firefighting so leadership gets its time back for strategy.
  • Scale the function as you grow, then either hand it to a full-time hire or taper out on a defined timeline.
  • Keep the work legible to leadership throughout, so there’s no black box when I leave.
Artifacts
  • A running ops function: planning cadence, operating rhythms, and a system of record
  • A defined spec for the permanent ops role, so hiring is against a real job, not a guess
  • Capacity and resourcing model leadership can run without you
  • Documentation and handoff plan so the function survives the transition
